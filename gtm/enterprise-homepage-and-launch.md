## Homepage Narrative

### The Problem

Most enterprises do not have an AI adoption problem.

They have an AI rollout problem.

One team has a strong Claude setup.
Another has Cursor rules and subagents.
Another has a few Codex plugins.
Another has internal CLIs and MCPs that only work in one environment.

The result is:

- standards drift
- duplicated effort
- weak install stories
- too much tribal knowledge
- too many "golden path" docs that no one follows

### The Shift

The right move is not just asking everyone to "use AI more."

The right move is packaging the best internal workflows so the org can actually adopt them consistently.

That means:

- skills
- hooks
- commands
- specialist agents
- runtime integrations
- install and verification paths

### The Pluxx Answer

Pluxx gives engineering organizations a cross-host delivery layer for those workflows.

Instead of maintaining separate internal setups for Claude Code, Cursor, Codex, and OpenCode, teams can:

1. define one maintained source project
2. encode internal best practices once
3. compile native outputs for the core four
4. validate installs and keep them in sync over time

### Why This Matters

This is how a platform team can help the org get:

- better agent adoption
- better coding hygiene
- better install trust
- higher throughput
- stronger developer experience

without turning the company into four separate AI tooling cultures.

## Launch Narrative

### Short Version

Most engineering organizations do not need more AI demos.

They need a way to turn their best internal AI workflows into something the whole org can actually install, use, and trust.

That is what Pluxx is becoming.

Pluxx started as a cross-host compiler for agent plugins.

That is still true.

But the bigger opportunity is clearer now: Pluxx can help platform and engineering teams package their internal agent operating model once and roll it out across Claude Code, Cursor, Codex, and OpenCode without rebuilding it four different times.

### Medium Launch Draft

The enterprise AI coding story is not just about models.

It is about distribution, guardrails, rollout, and trust.

Every serious engineering org ends up discovering the same thing:

- skills matter
- hooks matter
- internal CLIs matter
- MCPs matter
- install friction matters
- quality standards matter
- and host fragmentation matters

You can absolutely build a strong setup on one host.

The hard part is rolling that setup out across the rest of the organization without creating four different internal systems.

That is the problem Pluxx is built to solve.

Pluxx lets teams define one maintained source project for their agent workflows, compile native outputs for Claude Code, Cursor, Codex, and OpenCode, and verify that those installs are actually usable in the real host apps.

That makes Pluxx more than a generator.

It makes it a delivery layer for agent-first engineering standards.

The thing most teams underestimate about AI coding adoption is that the hard part is not the first great workflow.

The hard part is making that workflow portable, repeatable, and trustworthy across the rest of the org.

One team has a great Claude setup.

## Messaging Guardrails

Do:

- lead with rollout, standardization, and portability
- emphasize quality and developer experience, not just speed
- keep the core four explicit and tangible
- position Pluxx as one maintained source project plus native outputs

Do not:

- collapse the story into generic plugin generation
- imply that enterprises only care about marketplace packaging
- make it sound like Firecrawl or any single provider is the whole product
- oversell governance features that do not exist yet

- cross-host compilation

### Paid / enterprise story

The stronger paid wedge is not the compiler itself.

It is the organizational layer around it:

- rollout controls
- distribution/versioning
- centralized usage telemetry
- org pack management
- adoption analytics
- enterprise support and governance surfaces
